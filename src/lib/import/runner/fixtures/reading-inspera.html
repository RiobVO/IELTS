River Ferries of the Old Valley

For many centuries the wide river that cut through the valley could only be crossed by small wooden boats. Each village kept a boatman whose task was to move travellers and goods from one bank to the other. Before mechanical engines arrived, these crossings depended entirely on human effort and the strength of the current.

By the middle of the nineteenth century the first steam vessels began to replace the older rowing boats. They were faster and could carry heavier loads, but they were also expensive to run. Only the busiest crossings could afford them, so many quiet villages kept their traditional boats for another generation.

The arrival of the railway eventually reduced the importance of the ferries. Bridges were built at the main towns, and the boatmen slowly disappeared. Today only a single restored vessel remains, kept afloat by a local society as a reminder of a slower age.

Plants of the Dry Plateau

The plateau receives very little rain, and the plants that grow there have developed unusual ways of surviving. Some store water in thick stems, while others send roots deep below the surface to reach hidden moisture.

Researchers who study the region argue that these plants are far more varied than they first appear. A single slope may hold dozens of species, each adapted to a slightly different pocket of soil and shade.

Grazing animals have shaped the plateau as much as the climate. Where herds are common, only the toughest and least palatable plants survive, and the softer species retreat to rocky ledges that animals cannot reach.
